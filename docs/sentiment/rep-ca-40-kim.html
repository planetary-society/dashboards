Meeting Strategy

Lead with: Science as the Engine of Global Leadership

Lead with the Representative's own framing of a modern 'space race' where the U.S. must outcompete China and Russia. You should argue that cutting NASA's science budget is a direct threat to the technological superiority and R&D leadership she views as essential for national security. Given the thin direct SMD record, treat this as the best available opening rather than evidence of likely support.

Bridge issues:

  • National Security and Global Competition: "You have stated that the space race is only beginning; fully funding NASA's Science Mission Directorate is the best way to ensure the U.S. doesn't fall behind China in critical space-based research and development."
  • Local Research and Economic Impact: "NASA science is already at work in the 40th District, with Chapman University using data from instruments like ECOSTRESS to solve vital water management challenges for our state."

Watch out for:

  • Do not assume she is already committed to specific science funding increases; while she supports NASA broadly, she has not yet signed on to the $9 billion funding target for the Science Mission Directorate.
  • Do not oversell science-specific support when the public record is thin; keep the ask anchored in local value and broad NASA space science.

Expect this reaction: The office will likely be receptive to the 'space race' and local economic arguments, but they may remain non-committal on specific high-level funding requests given her cautious fiscal record on science-specific letters.

About Representative Young Kim (R-CA-40)

  • Earned a bachelor's degree in business administration from the University of Southern California.
  • Professional experience includes working as a financial analyst for First Interstate Bank, a controller for JK Sportswear Manufacturing, and owning a clothing industry business.
  • Served for 21 years as a community liaison and director of Asian affairs for U.S. Representative Ed Royce.
  • Advocated for increased education funding in science, technology, engineering, and math (STEM) as part of her congressional platform.
  • Served as a member of the California State Assembly from 2014 to 2016, becoming the first South Korean-born Republican woman elected to the state legislature.
