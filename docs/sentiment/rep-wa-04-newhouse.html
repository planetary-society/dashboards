Meeting Strategy

Lead with: NASA Science as a Tool for Rural Resiliency

Lead with the practical applications of NASA's science missions, specifically how satellite data and space biology benefit Washington's agricultural and research sectors. As a member of the Appropriations Committee and a farmer himself, you should frame NASA science as a driver of rural resiliency and innovation for local institutions like WSU and PNNL.

Bridge issues:

  • Agricultural and Wildfire Preparedness: "Your co-sponsorship of the SPACE Act shows you understand how critical NASA's satellite data is for helping our farmers manage water and prepare for wildfires."
  • Local Scientific Research: "The NASA-funded research at WSU and PNNL into how living organisms respond to space is a prime example of how space science drives innovation right here in the Yakima Valley and the Tri-Cities."
  • STEM and Workforce Development: "It was inspiring to see your support for Wapato High School students' work on fruit preservation for NASA; keeping that pipeline of science funding open ensures these students have careers to come home to."

Watch out for:

  • While he supports NASA's overall budget, he has emphasized fiscal discipline; avoid presenting science funding as a 'no-cost' priority and be prepared to discuss the return on investment for his district.
  • Avoid claiming he is an established champion for the Science Mission Directorate specifically, as he has not signed recent funding letters and his record is thin on specific divisions like Astrophysics or Heliophysics.

Expect this reaction: Expect a pragmatic and attentive response. He is likely to be supportive of the 'how it helps my district' angle but may pivot to broader fiscal restraint and the need for spending trade-offs within the Appropriations Committee.

  • You might find it useful to mention his former Communications Director, Will Boyington, who is now at NASA leadership, as a bridge to show the agency's value is well-understood by his inner circle.

About Representative Dan Newhouse (R-WA-04)

  • Earned a Bachelor of Science degree in agricultural economics from Washington State University in 1977.
  • Served as the director of the Washington State Department of Agriculture from 2009 to 2013.
  • Operates a 600-acre farm in Sunnyside, Washington, producing hops, tree fruit, grapes, and alfalfa.
  • Advocated for U.S. leadership in biotechnology and alternative proteins, including cultivated meat, to ensure national food security.
  • Served four terms in the Washington House of Representatives prior to his election to the U.S. House of Representatives in 2014.
