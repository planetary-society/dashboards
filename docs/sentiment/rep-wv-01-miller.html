Bottom Line

Likely Supportive (moderate confidence): Representative Miller views NASA primarily through the lens of national security, domestic manufacturing, and the future STEM workforce. While she is a member of the Planetary Science Caucus, her public record focuses heavily on the Artemis program and aerospace suppliers like Constellium in Ravenswood. Meeting Strategy

Lead with: Manufacturing the Future of Space Science

Lead with Representative Miller’s proven commitment to West Virginia's aerospace supply chain and her membership in the Planetary Science Caucus. You should frame NASA's science missions not just as research, but as a critical driver for the high-tech manufacturing and specialized materials industries located in her district.

Bridge issues:

  • Domestic Manufacturing and Supply Chains: "NASA's science missions require the same high-performance materials produced at the Constellium facility in Ravenswood, so supporting a robust science budget helps ensure steady demand for West Virginia’s specialized aerospace manufacturing."
  • National Security and Orbital Debris: "Your leadership on the ORBITS Act demonstrates how vital it is to protect our space assets; NASA's science programs provide the tracking and monitoring data essential for managing debris and maintaining U.S. dominance in orbit."
  • STEM Workforce Development: "By funding NASA’s science programs, we are creating the research opportunities and robotics challenges that inspire West Virginia students to pursue high-tech careers right here at home."

Watch out for:

  • Avoid overstating her current commitment to science funding; while she is a member of the Planetary Science Caucus, her absence from recent Science Mission Directorate budget letters suggests she has not yet made this a top fiscal priority.
  • Be prepared for the conversation to shift toward human exploration (Artemis) or defense; avoid framing science and human exploration as competing priorities, as she is a vocal advocate for the lunar mission.

Expect this reaction: The office is likely to be supportive of the STEM and manufacturing aspects of the pitch, but may be lukewarm or non-committal regarding specific funding increases for the Science Mission Directorate due to a lack of recent history with these specific budget requests.

Key Opportunities

  • Local Institution: Highlight the Constellium facility in Ravenswood, which produces critical materials for NASA's deep space missions, to connect science funding to local industrial jobs.2
  • Direct NASA Record: Acknowledge her membership in the Congressional Planetary Science Caucus as a starting point for deeper engagement on Science Mission Directorate funding.1
  • Adjacent Interest: Link NASA’s Earth and space-based monitoring capabilities to her interest in orbital debris remediation and space situational awareness.5
  • Adjacent Interest: Leverage her frequent participation in NASA-related STEM events to argue that robust science funding is essential for West Virginia's talent pipeline.3 7

Key Challenges

  • Evidence Gap: Her direct NASA science record is thin regarding specific funding for Earth Science, Heliophysics, and Astrophysics, with no recent signatures on SMD-specific budget letters.9 10
  • Evidence Gap: The district received $0 in direct NASA science obligations between FY2023 and FY2025, which may make broad science funding seem less like a local economic priority.11
  • Competing Priority: Space policy priorities are heavily skewed toward human exploration (Artemis) and national security, which could leave SMD programs competing for attention.5 7 4

Strategic Considerations

  • Consider mentioning how space science leadership prevents competitors like China from setting the technical standards and rules for the orbital environment.
  • You might find it useful to link NASA’s robotics missions to her frequent participation in state robotics championships, emphasizing that planetary rovers are the ultimate proving ground for that talent.
  • It could be worth exploring how NASA's research into power systems for deep space missions aligns with her legislative interests in fusion energy and advanced manufacturing.

About Representative Carol D. Miller (R-WV-01)

  • Serves as the U.S. Representative for West Virginia's 1st congressional district, having previously represented the 3rd district from 2019 to 2023.
  • Served twelve years in the West Virginia House of Delegates from 2007 to 2019, representing both the 15th and 16th districts.
  • Holds a bachelor's degree in history and political science from Columbia College in South Carolina.
  • Maintains a background in agriculture as a farmer and owner of a bison farm that she personally managed prior to her congressional service.
  • Worked as an educator, which included teaching manners classes to elementary school students in Huntington, West Virginia.
