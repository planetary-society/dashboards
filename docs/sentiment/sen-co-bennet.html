Meeting Strategy

Lead with: The 'Colorado Model' of Science and STEM

Lead by framing NASA space science as the cornerstone of Colorado's aerospace economy and the ultimate engine for the STEM workforce goals the Senator championed as a school superintendent. You should emphasize that missions like OSIRIS-REx and Lucy, built in Colorado, provide the 'high-reward' research environment he seeks to create through the NEED Act.

Bridge issues:

  • Education and STEM Workforce: "As a former school superintendent, you know that NASA science programs are more than just research—they are the primary tools we have to inspire the next generation of Colorado students to enter the STEM careers you've supported through the NEED Act."
  • International Partnerships and Leadership: "Just as your TASA and Quad Space Acts strengthen our diplomatic ties through space, robust funding for NASA planetary science ensures the U.S. remains the partner of choice for international missions with allies like Australia and Japan."
  • Local Economic Impact: "With over $720 million in recent NASA science obligations coming to Colorado, including major work at BAE and Lockheed, supporting the Science Mission Directorate directly protects thousands of high-tech jobs in our state."

Watch out for:

  • Do not mistake his recent 'Nay' vote on the FY26 funding bill for a lack of support for science; he was actually protesting cuts to atmospheric research at NCAR and likely wants to see even stronger investment.
  • While he is a longtime champion of the Orion capsule, keep the focus on the Science Mission Directorate to ensure the science payloads and robotic missions built in Colorado don't get overlooked.

Expect this reaction: The office is likely to be highly receptive and supportive. Given his recent signature on the FY27 Science Mission Directorate letter, expect staff to be well-versed in the budget toplines and interested in how these missions benefit Colorado's university and industry clusters.

Key Opportunities

  • Direct NASA Record: The Senator signed the FY27 letter calling for a $9 billion Science Mission Directorate budget, demonstrating a clear commitment to the Society's top funding priority.1
  • District Fiscal Impact: Colorado hosts major NASA science contracts, including nearly $500M with BAE Systems and over $1B in combined contracts with Lockheed Martin for missions like OSIRIS-REx and Lucy.9 2
  • Bipartisan Coalition: He has a proven track record of bipartisan cooperation on space policy, recently co-sponsoring bills to expand NASA's international partnerships with Taiwan and the Quad nations.4 7
  • Local Institution: Significant NASA grants to the University of Colorado and UCAR for atmospheric and heliophysics research provide a local hook for supporting diverse science divisions.10

Key Challenges

  • Competing Priority: He recently voted against a major funding bill because it lacked protections for local atmospheric research facilities, suggesting he may prioritize specific local institutions over broad NASA funding if they are in conflict.6
  • Evidence Gap: While he shows strong support for Earth science and planetary missions, his specific record on astrophysics and biological/physical sciences is relatively thin.11

Strategic Considerations

  • Consider mentioning NASA's Earth science data as a tool for the climate resilience and energy grid modernization the Senator prioritizes.
  • You might find it useful to highlight the role of the University of Colorado and UCAR in heliophysics and atmospheric research, connecting planetary science to his specific concern for Colorado's research institutions.
  • It could be worth mentioning that supporting the Science Mission Directorate budget is the best way to ensure the continued operation of the Lucy and OSIRIS-REx missions, which are major Colorado success stories.

About Senator Michael F. Bennet (D-CO)

  • Served as managing director for the Anschutz Investment Company for six years, where he led the reorganization of an oil company and the consolidation of movie theater chains.
  • Served as superintendent of Denver Public Schools from 2005 to 2009, overseeing student enrollment growth and improvements in graduation rates.
  • Held executive roles in local government as chief of staff to the Mayor of Denver from 2003 to 2005.
  • Served as counsel to the U.S. Deputy Attorney General during the Bill Clinton administration and worked as an aide to the Governor of Ohio.
  • Earned a Juris Doctor from Yale Law School, where he served as editor-in-chief of the Yale Law Journal, and a Bachelor of Arts in history from Wesleyan University.
