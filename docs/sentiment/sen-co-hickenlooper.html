Champion (high confidence): Senator Hickenlooper is a premier advocate for NASA science, viewing the agency as a 'force multiplier' for discovery through probes, rovers, and telescopes. As a geologist and member of the Space Subcommittee, he understands the technical value of planetary and Earth science, evidenced by his leadership on the ASCEND Act and Space RACE Act. Meeting Strategy

Lead with: The Science Force Multiplier and Colorado's Economic Engine

Lead with the Senator's own description of NASA as a 'force multiplier for science' and thank him for his leadership on the Space Subcommittee. You should emphasize that the $240 million in annual NASA science funding flowing into Colorado supports both groundbreaking discoveries and the state's premier aerospace workforce.

Bridge issues:

  • Climate and Earth Science Leadership: "Senator, your work on the ASCEND Act shows you understand how vital satellite data is for Earth science; robust funding for the Science Mission Directorate ensures we keep the 'probes and rovers' in flight that provide this essential data."
  • Aerospace Jobs and Innovation: "With major missions like OSIRIS-REx and Lucy being managed right here in Colorado, NASA science funding is a direct investment in our local economy and the high-tech jobs at companies like Lockheed Martin and BAE Systems."

Watch out for:

  • Be prepared for questions about 'balancing' science with human exploration; the Senator has expressed concern about how NASA manages both during 'fairly dramatic' budget environments.
  • Avoid treating his 'Nay' vote on H.R. 6938 as a lack of support for NASA; his consistent signatures on Science Mission Directorate budget letters are a much more accurate signal of his commitment to space science.

Expect this reaction: The office will likely be very receptive and knowledgeable, as the Senator is a 'champion' for these issues. Expect staff to focus on how science funding specifically benefits Colorado's research institutions and aerospace industry.

  • Competing Priority: Has expressed concern over how NASA balances scientific missions with exploration goals during 'fairly dramatic' budget environments.4
  • Evidence Gap: While strongly supportive of Earth and planetary science, his record is thinner on specific funding advocacy for the Heliophysics and Astrophysics divisions.4 2

Strategic Considerations

  • Consider mentioning his professional background as a geologist when discussing planetary science missions; he has a unique technical appreciation for the 'ground truth' these missions provide.
  • You might find it useful to bring up his Space RACE Act and ask how the proposed National Institute for Space Research could further integrate NASA's scientific goals with long-term exploration.
  • Think about whether to highlight the role of Colorado's universities, like CU Boulder or UCAR, which receive millions in NASA grants for heliophysics and atmospheric research.

About Senator John W. Hickenlooper (D-CO)

  • Holds a B.A. in English and a master's degree in geology from Wesleyan University.
  • Worked professionally as a petroleum geologist for Buckhorn Petroleum in Colorado during the early 1980s.
  • Co-founded the Wynkoop Brewing Company in 1988, establishing one of the first brewpubs in the United States.
  • Served as the 43rd mayor of Denver (2003–2011) and the 42nd governor of Colorado (2011–2019) before his election to the U.S. Senate.
  • Implemented the first methane-capture regulations for oil and gas companies in the U.S. and has expressed support for hydraulic fracturing based on his geological background.
