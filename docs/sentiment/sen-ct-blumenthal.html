Meeting Strategy

Lead with: Protecting the Future of Connecticut's Aerospace Alley

Lead by thanking the Senator for his direct support of the $9 billion funding request for NASA's Science Mission Directorate. Frame NASA science as the essential fuel for Connecticut's high-tech industrial base and the world-class research conducted at local universities like UConn and Wesleyan.

Bridge issues:

  • National Defense and Aerospace Manufacturing: "NASA science missions rely on the same specialized workforce and 'Aerospace Alley' expertise that you champion, ensuring Connecticut companies like Linde continue to lead in space operations and high-tech manufacturing."
  • STEM Education and Research: "With over $4 million in annual NASA science grants coming to Connecticut, researchers at UConn and Wesleyan are training the next generation of scientists while leading the world in understanding our climate and the origins of life."

Watch out for:

  • Avoid centering the conversation on the Collins Aerospace spacesuit contract. While the Senator previously championed it, the contract was recently descoped, making it a sensitive topic regarding local job stability.
  • Do not oversell the Senator's familiarity with specific planetary missions. While he supports the overall science budget, he has no recent public record regarding projects like Mars Sample Return or Europa Clipper.

Expect this reaction: The office is likely to be highly receptive and supportive. Senator Blumenthal has a strong record of signing budget support letters and clearly views NASA through the lens of Connecticut's economic and academic strength.

  • Direct NASA Record: Blumenthal signed the FY26 letter requesting $9 billion for the Science Mission Directorate, indicating direct support for your primary funding goals.1

  • Evidence Gap: There is a notable evidence gap regarding his views on specific uncrewed planetary science missions, such as Mars Sample Return or the Europa Clipper.9

About Senator Richard Blumenthal (D-CT)

  • Served in the United States Marine Corps Reserve from 1970 to 1976, attaining the rank of sergeant.
  • Held significant legal roles including U.S. Attorney for the District of Connecticut, administrative assistant to Senator Abraham Ribicoff, and law clerk to Supreme Court Justice Harry A. Blackmun.
  • Served as the Attorney General of Connecticut for 20 years (1991–2010), leading major litigation against the tobacco industry and Microsoft.
  • Earned a J.D. from Yale Law School, where he served as editor-in-chief of the Yale Law Journal, and an A.B. in government from Harvard College.
  • Advocated for scientific consensus on climate change and led legislative efforts to address online safety and harms children experience on social media platforms.
