Meeting Strategy

Lead with: Protecting the Public Interest and Massachusetts’ Scientific Leadership

Lead by recognizing the Senator as a 'Champion of Science' and thanking him for his direct legislative action to protect the Chandra X-ray Observatory. You should emphasize that a robust Science Mission Directorate budget is the only way to sustain the $500 million in NASA science investment flowing into Massachusetts research hubs like the Smithsonian and MIT.

Bridge issues:

  • Climate Change and Earth Science: "Senator, we know you have fought to protect climate data; supporting the full Science Mission Directorate budget ensures NASA’s Earth Science satellites continue providing the essential data needed to track sea-level rise and polar ice loss."
  • Astrophysics and Local Workforce: "By supporting a $9 billion topline for NASA science, you are directly protecting the high-tech workforce at the Chandra X-ray Observatory and the TESS project at MIT that keep Massachusetts at the forefront of discovery."

Watch out for:

  • Avoid framing NASA science primarily as a vehicle for commercial space partnerships, as the Senator is deeply skeptical of private interests 'stripping NASA for parts.'
  • Be prepared for the Senator to focus heavily on Earth science; acknowledge its importance but remind the office that current budget threats endanger the entire Science Mission Directorate portfolio.

Expect this reaction: Expect a highly supportive and knowledgeable response, as the Senator is a senior member of the Commerce Committee and a recent signer of the Science Mission Directorate support letter. He will likely agree with the need for funding but may express concern about the influence of commercial space lobbyists on the agency's core science mission.

Key Opportunities

  • Direct NASA Record: Introduced the 'Support for Astrophysical Observatories and National High-Energy Astrophysics Hubs Act' (S. 4044) to protect the Chandra X-ray Observatory and local workforce.1

  • Committee Position: As a senior member of the Senate Commerce Committee, he has direct oversight of NASA and has used this position to defend climate research data.5 1

Key Challenges

  • Evidence Gap: There is an evidence gap regarding the Senator's specific stances on planetary science, heliophysics, and biological and physical sciences, as his record is heavily weighted toward Earth science and astrophysics.5 1
  • Political Constraint: The Senator has expressed significant concern that commercial space interests may 'strip NASA for parts,' which could lead to skepticism of public-private partnerships in science missions.4 6

Strategic Considerations

  • You might find it useful to mention the TEMPO mission at the Smithsonian, which monitors air pollution, as it aligns with his career-long focus on environmental justice and public health.
  • Think about whether to highlight the Draper Laboratory’s work on lunar payloads to connect his interest in local high-tech manufacturing to the broader goals of planetary exploration.
  • Consider exploring how NASA's basic research grants to Boston University and Harvard support the next generation of the STEM workforce that he has championed for decades.

About Senator Edward J. Markey (D-MA)

  • Served in the United States Army Reserve from 1968 to 1973, attaining the rank of Specialist 4.
  • Earned a Bachelor of Arts from Boston College in 1968 and a Juris Doctor from Boston College Law School in 1972.
  • Chaired the House Select Committee on Energy Independence and Global Warming from 2007 to 2011 and authored the Green New Deal in the Senate.
  • Maintained a legislative focus on technology and science issues, including net neutrality, children's online privacy, and nuclear reactor safety.
  • Held a career in public service spanning the Massachusetts House of Representatives (1973–1976), the U.S. House of Representatives (1976–2013), and the U.S. Senate (2013–present).
