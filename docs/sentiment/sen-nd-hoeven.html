NASA Budget Support

  • FY2026 CJS Appropriations vote: Yea on H.R. 6938

Bottom Line

Uncertain (moderate confidence): Senator Hoeven is a key decision-maker as a member of the Senate Appropriations Committee, but his public record focuses heavily on defense-related space and STEM education rather than specific NASA science missions. While he supports the broad NASA budget, he has not signed recent letters specifically calling for increased Science Mission Directorate (SMD) funding. Advocates should bridge the conversation by highlighting the $1.3 million in NASA science grants currently supporting the University of North Dakota, particularly in Earth science and biological research. Emphasizing how these programs build the high-tech workforce he prioritizes is the most effective path to securing his support for the SMD topline. The public record shows broad NASA relevance but little recent, specific evidence on Science Mission Directorate funding priorities. This is a heuristic-heavy assessment based more on institutional and political context than on direct recent SMD evidence.

Meeting Strategy

Lead with: The Best Available Opening: Local Research and Appropriations Influence

Lead with the Senator’s critical role on the Appropriations Committee and the $1.3 million in NASA science obligations currently benefiting the University of North Dakota. You should emphasize that these specific science grants are vital for the state's high-tech ecosystem and academic prestige, framing NASA science as a local economic driver. Given the thin direct SMD record, treat this as the best available opening rather than evidence of likely support.

Bridge issues:

  • STEM Workforce Development: "Senator, NASA's Science Mission Directorate funding supports programs like the Space Grant Consortium that allow North Dakota students to intern at NASA centers and prepare for the high-tech careers you've championed."
  • Local Research Impact: "The University of North Dakota is currently using NASA science grants to improve snowfall prediction and study microbial risks in space, research that directly strengthens our state's scientific leadership."

Watch out for:

  • Avoid overselling the Senator's science-specific support; while he voted for the broad NASA budget, he has not yet signed on to specific Science Mission Directorate funding requests.
  • Be prepared for the office to pivot the conversation toward defense-related space projects or unmanned aerial systems (UAS), which are his primary aerospace priorities.

Expect this reaction: The office will likely be polite and supportive of NASA's broad mission but non-committal on specific science funding levels, as they prioritize defense-aligned space spending and local ROI.

Key Opportunities

  • Committee Position: As a member of the Senate Committee on Appropriations, Senator Hoeven has direct influence over the final funding levels for NASA's Science Mission Directorate.2 3
  • Local Institution: The University of North Dakota is a major beneficiary of NASA science funding, receiving grants for Earth science (GPM/CloudSat) and biological research in closed habitats.3 9
  • Direct NASA Record: Senator Hoeven voted in favor of H.R. 6938, indicating a baseline level of support for the broader NASA budget and its mission objectives.1

Key Challenges

  • Evidence Gap: The public record shows broad NASA relevance but little recent, specific evidence on Science Mission Directorate funding priorities.7 6 1
  • Competing Priority: The Senator's primary space focus is on the Space Development Agency and military LEO missions, which may compete for attention with non-defense NASA science priorities.7 8

About Senator John Hoeven (R-ND)

  • Served as the 31st Governor of North Dakota from 2000 to 2010 before being elected to the U.S. Senate in 2010.
  • Extensive background in the banking industry, including serving as president and CEO of the state-owned Bank of North Dakota and executive vice president of First Western Bank & Trust.
  • Holds an MBA from the Kellogg School of Management at Northwestern University and a B.A. from Dartmouth College.
  • Directed the development of a multi-resource energy program for North Dakota as governor, focusing on expanding the state's energy production and export sectors.
  • Advocates for a long-term transition to alternative fuels while supporting increased domestic oil drilling and infrastructure projects like the Keystone Pipeline.
