Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN)

Senator | R | TN

NASA Budget Support

  • FY2026 CJS Appropriations vote: Yea on H.R. 6938

Bottom Line

Uncertain (moderate confidence): Senator Blackburn is a supporter of NASA's broad mission and the Artemis program, primarily through the lens of national security, global competitiveness, and Tennessee's STEM workforce. While she voted for broad NASA budget support, she has not yet signed onto specific funding requests for the Science Mission Directorate. Advocates should focus on the significant NASA science investments at Tennessee institutions like Vanderbilt and the UT Space Institute, framing space science as a critical component of U.S. technological leadership against adversaries. Be prepared for potential skepticism regarding Earth Science programs due to her public stance on climate change. The public record shows broad NASA relevance but little recent, specific evidence on Science Mission Directorate funding priorities. This is a heuristic-heavy assessment based more on institutional and political context than on direct recent SMD evidence.

Meeting Strategy

Lead with: Tennessee’s Role in the Global Space Race

Lead with the Senator's influential role on the Senate Commerce Committee and the $88 million in NASA science funding recently obligated to Tennessee institutions. You should frame NASA space science as the essential R&D engine that fuels the Tennessee workforce and ensures the U.S. maintains a technological edge over adversaries like China. Given the thin direct SMD record, treat this as the best available opening rather than evidence of likely support.

Bridge issues:

  • STEM Workforce and Education: "NASA science missions at Vanderbilt and the UT Space Institute are directly training the next generation of Tennessee engineers and scientists that you have championed through the Building Blocks of STEM Act."
  • National Security and Global Competition: "To stay ahead of the 'New Axis of Evil' in space, we must robustly fund the NASA science missions that develop the advanced sensors and autonomous technologies required for our national defense."

Watch out for:

  • Avoid assuming the Senator is a committed champion of the Science Mission Directorate; while she supports the broad NASA budget, she has not yet signed on to specific science funding requests.
  • Do not oversell science-specific support when the public record is thin; keep the ask anchored in local value and broad NASA space science.

Expect this reaction: The office will likely be receptive to the economic and security arguments but may remain non-committal on specific Science Mission Directorate funding levels. Expect them to pivot the conversation toward Artemis or the Space Force.

Key Opportunities

  • District Fiscal Impact: Tennessee receives significant NASA science funding, including over $88M in obligations (FY23-25) and major research grants at Vanderbilt University for planetary and Earth science missions.2
  • Committee Position: As a member of the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, she has a direct platform to influence NASA's scientific and exploration priorities.4
  • Local Institution: She has advocated for utilizing the UT Space Institute and Tennessee Space Grant Consortium to coordinate aerospace innovation, providing a bridge to discuss NASA science's role in regional development.3

Key Challenges

  • Evidence Gap: The public record shows broad NASA relevance but little recent, specific evidence on Science Mission Directorate funding priorities.2
  • Ideological Barrier: Her public rejection of the scientific consensus on climate change presents a major ideological barrier to supporting NASA's Earth Science and climate monitoring missions.1
  • Competing Priority: Her space policy focus is heavily weighted toward military applications, such as the Space National Guard, which may compete with civil science for attention and resources.7 5

About Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN)

  • Earned a Bachelor of Science in home economics from Mississippi State University in 1974, which she attended on a 4-H scholarship.
  • Maintained a private sector career as the owner of Marketing Strategies, a promotion-event management firm, and previously served as a sales manager for the Times Mirror Company.
  • Chaired the Select Investigative Panel on fetal tissue research and has focused legislative efforts on technology policy, including online safety and anti-competitive behavior in app markets.
  • Rejects the scientific consensus on climate change and has publicly challenged the urgency and causes of global warming.
  • Served as executive director of the Tennessee Film, Entertainment, and Music Commission from 1995 to 1997 prior to her election to the Tennessee Senate.
